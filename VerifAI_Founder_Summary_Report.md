## What VerifAI Is

A forensic detection platform that determines whether images and videos were generated by AI. It serves three users: everyday people who need a quick answer, businesses that need API-scale detection, and legal professionals who need court-admissible evidence reports.

The goal is to become the internationally recognised standard for AI-generated content detection — accepted by courts, news organisations, governments, and social platforms.

## How the Detection Actually Works

We are not guessing. We are measuring physical evidence in the pixels and file data. Think of it like forensic document analysis — a forensic expert does not feel that a document is fake, they measure specific things: ink chemistry, paper fibres, printing patterns. We do the same with digital media.

### The Six Detection Signals

**1. Frequency analysis (FFT)**
Real cameras capture light through a lens onto a sensor. This creates a specific statistical pattern in pixel data that is measurable with standard maths (Fast Fourier Transform). AI generators predict pixel values rather than capture light, leaving a completely different pattern. No AI needed to detect this — just mathematics.

**2. Neural classifier (CNN + Vision Transformer)**
Two neural networks running in parallel. The CNN looks at local texture — skin pores, hair strands, background detail. The Vision Transformer looks at global patterns — whether lighting is consistent across the whole image, whether shadows make physical sense. Together they catch what each misses alone.

**3. Noise fingerprinting**
Every camera sensor adds random noise to photos. AI models also add noise, but it is structured and repeating in ways real sensor noise never is. Each AI model family (Midjourney, Flux, Sora) leaves a unique noise signature — like a manufacturer fingerprint. We build a database of these fingerprints and match against it.

**4. Metadata forensics**
Real photos carry camera data baked in — shutter speed, lens model, GPS, serial number. AI images have none of this because no camera existed. This requires zero AI to detect — just reading the file header.

**5. Facial and anatomy analysis**
Measurable geometric facts: hands with wrong finger counts, eye reflections that do not match between left and right, teeth that are too symmetric, skin texture that repeats. These are not opinions — they are measurements.

**6. Temporal consistency (video only)**
In real video, objects maintain consistent shape, texture, and position across frames. In AI video, textures shift subtly between frames in ways optical flow analysis can measure precisely.

### What Makes Us Succeed

Three things, in order of importance:

**Combining signals.** Any single signal can be fooled. Combining six independent signals — each looking at different evidence — makes it very hard to fool all six simultaneously. This is the ensemble approach.

**The fingerprint database.** Over time we build a database of noise signatures per AI model version. Midjourney v6 has a different fingerprint from Flux 1.1, which differs from Sora. When we can say "this image matches Midjourney v6 at 89% confidence," that is court-level evidence. This database cannot be bought — it is built by collecting samples, and it becomes more valuable every month.

**Explainability.** Competitors give a score. We give the evidence. "Frequency anomaly detected, metadata absent, noise matches Midjourney v6" is a statement that can be cross-examined in court. A black-box score cannot.

## Open Source Models That Cut Detection Costs

Yes — several strong open source tools exist that reduce what you need to build from scratch.

### For image classification
- **EfficientNet (Google)** — Apache 2.0 licence. Pre-trained on ImageNet, fine-tune on your dataset. This is the CNN backbone we use. Free.
- **ViT (Vision Transformer, Google)** — Apache 2.0. The global pattern detector. Available via Hugging Face. Free.
- **timm library** — PyTorch Image Models. 600+ pre-trained vision models. MIT licence. Free.

### For face and anatomy analysis
- **InsightFace** — MIT licence. Best open source face analysis available. Handles partial faces, eye geometry, skin analysis. Free.
- **MediaPipe (Google)** — Apache 2.0. Hand detection, pose estimation. Free.

### For noise and frequency analysis
- **NumPy / SciPy** — BSD licence. Standard maths libraries for FFT analysis. Free.
- **PyWavelets** — MIT licence. Wavelet frequency analysis. Free.

### For video processing
- **PyAV** — BSD licence. Video decoding. Free.
- **RAFT (optical flow)** — BSD licence. State of the art motion analysis. Free.

### For similarity search (fingerprint matching)
- **FAISS (Facebook AI)** — MIT licence. Billion-scale vector similarity search. Free.

### Pre-trained detection models you can start with
- **Grounding DINO** — Apache 2.0. Object detection useful for anatomy checks.
- **CNNDetection (Wang et al.)** — Research licence. Specifically trained to detect GAN images. Good starting baseline.
- **UnivFD** — Research code. Universal fake image detector. Good for understanding what signals matter.

### Cost impact
Using open source models instead of paid APIs saves roughly $0.03-0.08 per scan at scale. At 100,000 scans per month that is $3,000-8,000 saved monthly. The only cost is your compute time to run them, which at CPU level is nearly free for early testing.

## Stage by Stage Build Order

**Stage 1 — Weeks 1-6**
Python only. FFT module + basic CNN classifier. Test on 1,000 images. Pass when accuracy hits 70%.

**Stage 2 — Weeks 7-12**
FastAPI endpoint + Supabase database + Celery job queue. Pass when you can curl the API and get a verdict back.

**Stage 3 — Weeks 13-20**
Next.js frontend + real-time results + user accounts + ensemble scoring. Pass when you trust the results yourself at 85%+.

**Stage 4 — Weeks 21-28**
Heatmap output + plain English explanations + Stripe billing + developer API. Pass when you have 50 paying users.

**Stage 5 — Weeks 29-40**
Video processing + facial analysis + forensic report generator + model attribution. Pass when a lawyer accepts your report as evidence.

**Stage 6 — Months 11-24**
Retraining pipeline + browser extension + enterprise API + NIST submission + industry standard positioning.

## Documents Needed — Now vs Later

### Need Now (before you write a line of code)
None of the legal documents are urgent before you have something built. Focus on building first.

### Need Before First External Money or Partner
- **Founder agreement** — not applicable, solo founder
- **Incorporation documents** — register the company before taking any money
- **IP assignment agreement** — assigns all your work to the company, not to you personally
- **Terms of service + privacy policy** — needed before public launch

### Need Before Raising Seed Funding
- **Cap table** — even as a solo founder, track your equity from day one
- **SAFE note template** — the instrument you will use to raise at idea stage
- **Investment memo** — 2-3 pages explaining the opportunity for investors
- **Pitch deck** — 12-15 slides

### Need When You Hire
- **Employee contract**
- **Board resolution template**

### Need When You Have Revenue
- **Shareholders agreement** — when you have investors
- **Financial statements** — formal P&L and balance sheet for investors
- **Business plan** — formal document for grant applications and some investors

## What You Can Raise at Idea Stage

At idea stage with no product, you raise on a **SAFE note** (Simple Agreement for Future Equity). This is not a loan and not equity — it converts to equity at your next priced round.

**Realistic raise at idea stage: $25,000 to $150,000**

This comes from friends, family, angels who know you personally, or angel networks focused on African tech founders.

**Valuation cap on the SAFE: $2-4M**

This means your early investors convert their SAFE into equity at a maximum of $2-4M valuation when you raise your first priced round. So if you raise $50,000 on a $3M cap SAFE and then raise a seed round at $8M valuation, your early investor converts at $3M (not $8M) — giving them more shares as a reward for backing you early.

**What you need to show to raise on a SAFE at idea stage:**
- A clear technical explanation of how the detection works (the six signals)
- Evidence you understand the market (the competitor analysis)
- A working prototype or proof of concept — even a Python script with 70% accuracy is enough
- A credible founder — relevant background in tech, ML, or forensics helps enormously
- A specific ask — "I am raising $75,000 on a $3M cap SAFE to fund 6 months of development and get to a working product with 85% accuracy"

**What you do NOT need at idea stage:**
Revenue, paying users, a finished product, or a co-founder.
